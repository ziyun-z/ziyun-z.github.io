No Rehearsal, No Internet, No Map

Ziyun Zhao · 23 February 2026 · 11 min read

In the 2026 CMG Spring Festival Gala, Unitree's WuBOT put G1 and H2 humanoids on stage with students from the Henan Tagou Martial Arts School: table-vault parkour, three-meter aerial flips, chained single-leg backflips, a seven-and-a-half-rotation airflare, staff work with dexterous hands, cluster repositioning at up to 4 m/s. MagicLab's Z1 landed a continuous Thomas 360. Noetix ran backflips inside twelve square meters of stage in a comedy sketch. Galbot, in a short film, folded clothes.

Twelve months earlier, on the same stage, the previous generation twirled handkerchiefs and needed a human hand to walk off.

That delta is real, and it makes me think about the future of robotics and physical intelligence.

I. There was a rehearsal. There is never a rehearsal.

Give the Gala its due first, because the easy dismissal is wrong.

This was a live broadcast to the largest television audience on Earth. No second take. Moving props, hundreds of human dancers, a physical stage with changing surfaces, dozens of robots holding formation while airborne.

Now the other half. Before that live take: hundreds of millions of training iterations in simulation, then fine-tuning on hardware. A launcher platform custom-built to give the robots the height for those flips. Every agent on that stage, human and robot, executing a plan known in advance to every other agent.

So what did we learn? We learned these systems are robust to variance within a known distribution. That is a hard property and they earned it. What we did not learn — because the Gala could not have tested it — is whether they generalize to a distribution they've never seen.

Is the robot ready for real life? I think that's the wrong question; asking it that way produces either hype or dismissal, both lazy. The better question:

How much of real life is rehearsable?

A factory is rehearsable. Fixed layout, known parts, repeatable task, and a reward function you can literally write down because it's already a KPI someone is measured on.

A home is not rehearsable. The layout changes. The objects are unbounded. A cat walks through. The task is underspecified — "clean up" — and the success criterion lives in someone's head and moves with their mood. There is no reward function, only a person's taste. And a bad rollout isn't a retry with backoff; it's a broken heirloom.

The Gala stage is much closer to a factory than to a home. So the correct read is: robots are ready for environments you can rehearse — a big, real, valuable market — and that tells you almost nothing about the other one.

II. Robotics hasn't had its BERT moment. It hasn't had word2vec.

This is the part I can't stop thinking about.

What NLP had that robotics doesn't

Word2vec in 2013. BERT in 2018 — the moment one pretrained representation started transferring across nearly every downstream task, and the field stopped hand-building features. GPT-3 in 2020. Then the deluge.

Ask what made BERT possible. Not just the transformer. The internet already existed. Trillions of tokens of real human language, produced by billions of people for their own reasons, requiring nothing from us but a crawler. GPT-3 trained on roughly 300 billion tokens that nobody collected for the purpose of training GPT-3. Language data was the exhaust — a byproduct of humans doing what they were already doing.

Robotics has no exhaust.

Every trajectory must be manufactured: a physical machine, in physical space, at one-times real time, usually with a human teleoperator, in a lab someone pays rent on. It doesn't accumulate as a side effect of anything.

And it doesn't pool the way text does. An English sentence from Reddit and one from a novel are the same substrate. A trajectory from one robot's arm is not a trajectory from another's — different kinematics, sensors, action space, contact geometry. Cross-embodiment transfer is an open research problem precisely because it does not just work.

So the pretraining substrate that made everything in NLP possible does not exist — and unlike in NLP, it was never quietly accumulating in the background waiting for someone to notice. My read: robotics is somewhere pre-word2vec. No general representation that transfers, no "just fine-tune the foundation policy." Task-specific systems, trained per embodiment, extremely good at what they were trained on. That's 2012 NLP with better hardware.

"But we have simulation"

Half right. Understanding which half is the most important technical point here.

Simulation gives you dynamics. It does not give you contact.

The dynamics half is real, and it's why the Gala looked the way it looked. A humanoid in a ballistic arc — airborne, no contact, gravity and inertia only — is a rigid-body problem that physics engines model nearly exactly. Randomize a little, fine-tune on hardware a little, and it transfers. You can run a hundred million iterations overnight because falling over in sim is free. That is precisely why the airflare was achievable, and precisely why it was the thing they chose to show.

Then the robot touches something, and you fall off a cliff. Contact is where physics engines stop being physics engines and start being approximations with tuning knobs:

  • Friction is non-smooth. Stick-slip is a discontinuity. Solvers relax it into something tractable, and the error isn't small unbiased noise — it's systematic distortion at exactly the moment that matters.
  • Contact is stiff and chaotic. A small error in friction coefficient doesn't give you a slightly different grasp. The object slips, or it doesn't. Errors don't average out; they bifurcate.
  • Deformables are worse by an order of magnitude. Clothes, food, hair, cables, rice, flour. No compact state representation, no cheap accurate solver, and material parameters that are unmeasurable in practice.
  • Tactile sensing has no simulator worth the name, because you can't simulate what a touch sensor would read when you can't simulate the physics it's sensing.

"But world models"

The hope: don't write the physics down — learn it from video, the way we learned language from text. I want this to work. I don't think it rescues contact.

Video contains appearance, not force. There's no channel in an RGB frame for friction, mass, or compliance. The mapping from forces to pixels is many-to-one: a hand resting on a table and a hand pressing hard on it look identical. You cannot invert that. It's under-determination, not a data-scale problem you fix with more video.

Plausibility is cheap; physics isn't. A model producing a gorgeous ten-second video is doing something categorically easier than one predicting contact forces two hundred steps deep in a closed control loop. Video generation is graded by human eyes. Control is graded by physics.

My guess: world models will be genuinely transformative for the high-level layer — affordances, planning, what could plausibly happen next — and will not solve low-level contact. Real progress. It just means the touch problem, the one standing between us and every domestic and dexterous application, stays open.

Which leaves the unglamorous answer

In language, money bought compute and compute bought capability. In robotics, money buys robots and rooms and people, and those scale linearly and slowly. That's a fundamentally different scaling law.

III. The easy ten-year forecast, and the impossible three-year one

Today is clear. Bodies are athletic, cheap, shipping. Brains aren't ready. Locomotion is largely won; manipulation, contact, and open-world reasoning are not. Factories yes, homes no.

Ten years out is also clear. There will be capable general-purpose machines doing physical labor at scale. The economics are too enormous for it not to happen, and no law of nature stands in the way. Humanoid form probably wins in human-built environments — not because it's optimal, but because our doors and tools were built around a body plan, and copying the body is cheaper than rebuilding the world.

What I can't tell is the path between those two points. The path depends on which bottleneck breaks first, and the candidates have wildly different time constants and produce wildly different worlds:

  • The grind. Nothing breaks. Data accumulates linearly with fleet size, capability accretes vertical by vertical, each with bespoke data collection. No BERT moment, ever. Value goes to vertical integrators who own one niche end to end.
  • The flywheel ignites. Fleets get large enough that real-world data compounds, and around 2029–2031 someone trains a policy that actually transfers across tasks and embodiments. Value goes to whoever owns the fleet.
  • Simulation cracks. Someone solves differentiable contact and deformables well enough that sim-trained manipulation transfers without real-world fine-tuning.
  • Video transfer works. Manipulation priors learned from humans on camera; robotics gets its internet secondhand. Unclear whether force can be recovered from pixels. Largest single unlock available if it does.

V. Why this is still the most exciting problem I know

Everything above is a list of reasons this is hard. I don't read that as pessimism. A field where the obstacles are legible — no pretraining corpus, no faithful contact simulator, no shortcut around collecting real experience at one-times real time — is in far better shape than a field that doesn't yet know what's blocking it.

The other reason I'm optimistic is more selfish: in language, the scaling moment is behind us. In robotics, it is still ahead. Whatever the equivalent of 2018 turns out to be — the flywheel, the simulator, the transfer from human video — it hasn't happened yet, and the people who make it happen are, right now, working on something that doesn't look like a breakthrough.

Which is why I'd bet the real ChatGPT moment for robotics won't look like the Gala at all. It won't be a backflip. It'll be a robot in a room nobody rehearsed, handed a task nobody scripted, doing something completely unremarkable — and getting it right.

I'd very much like to be in that room.

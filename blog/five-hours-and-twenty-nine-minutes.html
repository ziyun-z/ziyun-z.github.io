Five Hours and Twenty-Nine Minutes

Ziyun Zhao · 8 June 2025 · 7 min read

I just watched Carlos Alcaraz do something I'll be thinking about for a long time.

Two sets down to Jannik Sinner in the Roland Garros final. Then championship point — not once but three times, at 3–5, love–forty on his own serve in the fourth set. One clean Sinner swing away from losing the whole thing. And he came back. Five hours and twenty-nine minutes after the first ball, the longest French Open final ever played, he won it: 4–6, 6–7, 6–4, 7–6, 7–6.

I sat there long after the trophy ceremony, not really thinking about tennis anymore. Or rather, thinking about tennis the way I usually do — as a way of thinking about everything else.

Why I love this game

Tennis is a lonely sport, and I mean that as a compliment. There are no teammates to hide behind and, crucially, no clock to run out. You cannot protect a lead by killing time. You cannot sit on the ball. Being up two sets guarantees you exactly nothing, because the only way the match ends is by winning the final point, and your opponent has every right to keep refusing to give it to you. It's one of the few contests left where "nearly over" and "over" are separated by an unbridgeable gap.

Even an amateur match gives you a miniature version of what the pros feel: the one where your legs are gone by the third set and the loudest opponent on the court is the voice in your own head. Sports compress all of that into a couple of hours and make the stakes honest. No spin, no committee, no reframing the narrative afterward. The scoreboard doesn't care about your résumé. I find that clarifying, and a little addictive.

In praise of the human in the chair

Here's a detail I love. As technology quietly takes over professional sport, Roland Garros is the last Grand Slam still using human line judges. The Australian Open, Wimbledon, and the US Open have all switched to electronic line calling — cameras, a synthetic voice, no argument. Paris still puts people in chairs to call the lines, and still has an umpire who climbs down from the chair to squint at the mark the ball leaves in the clay.

The obvious answer is: of course you automate it. The machine is more accurate. Fewer blown calls, less controversy, more fairness for the players whose careers hang on these margins. It's genuinely hard to argue against.

And yet I'm not sure the machine is the whole answer, and it's worth being honest about why. Part of what makes clay clay is that the evidence is physical. The ball leaves a mark, and two humans lean over a smudge in the dirt and have to agree on what actually happened. Automate the call and you gain accuracy — that's real, and it matters. But you lose something too: the fallibility, the judgment that could go either way, the moment a player has to decide whether to trust the call or fight it. That friction isn't a bug in the spectacle. It's part of the spectacle.

So the interesting question was never "can we automate this." It's "should we, and what quietly disappears when we do." I sit with that question a lot in my actual work. Automating a decision usually makes it more correct. It does not automatically make the thing more alive. The right call depends entirely on what the thing is for. A courtroom should optimize for getting it right. A game is partly theater. Knowing which one you're building — correctness machine or living drama — is most of the skill, and it's a lot easier to state than to actually get right.

Victory belongs to the most tenacious

Stenciled onto the walls of Court Philippe-Chatrier is a line: "Victory belongs to the most tenacious." In the original French, "La victoire appartient au plus opiniâtre." It's usually traced back to Napoleon, but the stadium borrowed it from the man it's named after — Roland Garros, the World War I aviator, who liked it enough to have it painted on the propellers of his plane. There's something fitting about a quote about endurance riding on the part of the machine that has to keep spinning or everything falls out of the sky.

Watching the final, the line kept coming back to me, because it explains the result better than talent does. Alcaraz didn't win because he was faster or cleaner on the day — for long stretches, Sinner was the better player, and the scoreline says so. Alcaraz won because he was still there in the sixth hour. He won on tenacity, on the refusal to let the match end, on being the one who was still standing when the other guy's certainty finally cracked.

Sometimes I find myself in a position that just isn't good — behind, stuck, out of rhythm — and the hard part isn't recognizing that I need to climb out. It's that I may have already tried, and failed, maybe more than once. That's exactly the moment the line on the wall is for. Not talent, not speed, not some clever escape. Just the discipline to keep doing the right thing, point after point, long after it stopped feeling like it was working. I want to keep reminding myself of that.

The match ends. The clay gets swept. The mark the ball made in the dirt disappears by the next morning. But the line on the wall stays exactly where it is, waiting for the next person who's down two sets and championship points, deciding whether they're done.

Not the fastest. The most tenacious.
